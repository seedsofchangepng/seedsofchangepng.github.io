Title: The Parenting Path: Raising Children for a Changed PNG

Subtitle: A National Call to Transform Our Future Through Parenting

Introduction

Papua New Guinea is blessed with a rich cultural heritage, deep spiritual roots, and a vibrant population. But despite our potential, we face serious challenges—youth crime, broken homes, domestic violence, school dropouts, and moral decline. These are not just political or economic issues. At the heart of many of these problems lies one root cause: the breakdown of effective parenting.

Parenting is the foundation of nation-building. Every leader, every teacher, every worker, every citizen starts their journey in a home. What they learn in their early years shapes the kind of person they become. The home is the first school. The parent is the first teacher. And parenting is the first leadership role in any nation.

This guidebook is a comprehensive tool to help parents, caregivers, teachers, and community leaders raise a generation of emotionally strong, morally sound, creative, and responsible citizens. Together, we can lay a new foundation for a better Papua New Guinea.

“Train up a child in the way he should go, and when he is old he will not depart from it.” — Proverbs 22:6

Chapter 1: Understanding Parenting

What is Parenting?

Parenting is the lifelong journey of raising a child with love, discipline, support, and guidance. It includes meeting the child’s physical needs, nurturing their emotional well-being, guiding their intellectual development, and shaping their moral compass. Parenting involves teaching life skills, beliefs, attitudes, and behaviors necessary for the child to become a healthy, responsible adult.

The Purpose of Parenting

  • To prepare children to become responsible adults: Equipping them with the tools to make wise decisions, build healthy relationships, and contribute to their communities.

  • To help children discover their God-given potential: Recognizing each child's unique gifts and helping them develop those gifts.

  • To create a safe and loving environment: A home where children feel protected, heard, and valued.

  • To teach love, faith, discipline, and moral values: Instilling foundational virtues that guide children through life.

“Fathers, do not provoke your children to anger, but bring them up in the discipline and instruction of the Lord.” — Ephesians 6:4

Types of Parenting Styles

  1. Authoritarian – High control, low warmth. Parents demand obedience without discussion. Often leads to fear and rebellion.

  2. Permissive – Low control, high warmth. Parents are indulgent and avoid discipline, leading to lack of boundaries.

  3. Neglectful – Low control, low warmth. Parents are uninvolved, resulting in children feeling abandoned or unloved.

  4. Authoritative – Balanced control and warmth. Parents set clear expectations with compassion and explain rules. This is the most effective style, fostering respect, confidence, and responsibility.

Chapter 2: The PNG Parenting Reality

Common Issues:

  • Physical punishment as the primary discipline method: Discipline often involves beatings, leading to fear instead of respect.

  • Absent fathers and overburdened mothers: Many children grow up without consistent fatherly guidance.

  • Little emotional connection between parents and children: Affection and communication are often lacking.

  • Trauma passed from generation to generation: Unhealed pain becomes part of the child’s emotional inheritance.

  • Lack of positive role models: Many parents lack examples of effective parenting.

  • Cultural silence on emotional issues: Important emotional discussions are often avoided.

The Effects:

  • Children grow up with fear, insecurity, and confusion.

  • Youth rebellion and disrespect for authority.

  • Poor academic performance and low self-esteem.

  • Early pregnancies and substance abuse.

  • Breakdown in social and family structures.

Chapter 3: The Foundations of Good Parenting

1. Love and Emotional Safety

Children need to know they are loved unconditionally. This means spending time with them, affirming them with words, showing physical affection, and being emotionally available. A safe emotional environment allows children to grow with confidence.

“As a father has compassion on his children, so the Lord has compassion on those who fear Him.” — Psalm 103:13

2. Positive Discipline

Discipline should guide, not punish. Instead of beating or shouting, use calm explanations, clear consequences, and natural results. Encourage good behavior through praise and correct bad behavior with patience and firmness.

“Whoever spares the rod hates their children, but the one who loves their children is careful to discipline them.” — Proverbs 13:24

3. Consistency and Structure

Children need routines to feel secure. Establish regular mealtimes, bedtimes, and rules. Be consistent in enforcing boundaries. Inconsistent discipline confuses children and encourages misbehavior.

4. Modeling Behavior

Children learn more from what parents do than what they say. Show kindness, honesty, forgiveness, and respect in your daily life. If you want your child to be honest, you must also be honest.

“Set an example for the believers in speech, in conduct, in love, in faith and in purity.” — 1 Timothy 4:12

5. Spiritual and Moral Teaching

Teach your children about God, prayer, and faith. Read the Bible with them, share lessons from traditional wisdom, and discuss right and wrong openly. Encourage virtues such as gratitude, humility, courage, and compassion.

“These commandments that I give you today are to be on your hearts. Impress them on your children.” — Deuteronomy 6:6–7

Chapter 4: Stages of Parenting

Early Childhood (0–5 years)

  • Children’s brains develop quickly during this time. They are like sponges, absorbing language, behavior, and emotion.

  • Engage with them through talking, reading stories, singing songs, and playful learning.

  • Provide consistent affection and set simple rules.

Middle Childhood (6–12 years)

  • Children begin to understand responsibility, fairness, and teamwork.

  • Assign them household chores and involve them in group activities.

  • Stay connected with their school life and friends.

  • Teach problem-solving and decision-making skills.

Teenage Years (13–19 years)

  • Teenagers seek identity, independence, and purpose.

  • Respect their opinions and listen actively.

  • Set clear boundaries with explanations, not threats.

  • Offer guidance and mentorship. Be a friend, but also a firm guardian.

“Don’t let anyone look down on you because you are young, but set an example for the believers.” — 1 Timothy 4:12

Chapter 5: Role of Mothers and Fathers

Mothers:

  • Provide daily emotional care, comfort, and moral guidance.

  • Encourage learning and build a sense of self-worth.

  • Help develop communication and empathy.

Fathers:

  • Provide protection, direction, and spiritual leadership.

  • Discipline with love and spend quality time teaching life lessons.

  • Their involvement builds the child’s confidence and identity, especially for sons and daughters alike.

Both parents must work together and support each other. Where one parent is missing, relatives or community members must step in with love and responsibility.

Chapter 6: Healing Parents to Raise Healthy Children

No parent is perfect, but many carry unhealed wounds from their own childhood. These wounds affect how they relate to their children.

Steps to Heal:

  1. Acknowledge past wounds: Reflect on your upbringing and identify areas of pain.

  2. Seek spiritual and emotional healing: Find support in faith, trusted friends, church groups, or professional counselors.

  3. Forgive and ask for forgiveness: Release bitterness towards those who hurt you and apologize to your children when needed.

  4. Learn new parenting skills: Read books, attend parenting seminars, or join community learning groups.

“He heals the brokenhearted and binds up their wounds.” — Psalm 147:3

Chapter 7: Culture, Faith, and Parenting in PNG

PNG culture has many strengths: community-based child rearing, storytelling, respect for elders, and traditional wisdom. These values can be harmonized with Biblical faith and modern understanding of child development.

How to Integrate:

  • Revive traditional stories and proverbs that teach moral lessons.

  • Use church and cultural ceremonies to teach family roles and virtues.

  • Blend ancestral wisdom with faith, emphasizing love, discipline, and responsibility.

“Listen to your father who gave you life, and do not despise your mother when she is old.” — Proverbs 23:22

Our culture and faith are not enemies—they are allies when rooted in truth and love.

Chapter 8: The Community's Role

No parent can raise a child alone. The whole community must come together.

  • Start parenting support groups in churches, schools, or villages.

  • Train teachers, pastors, and community leaders in child development.

  • Host community seminars on parenting, trauma healing, and positive discipline.

  • Encourage elder mentorship, where older couples guide young parents.

“Carry each other’s burdens, and in this way you will fulfill the law of Christ.” — Galatians 6:2

Chapter 9: Practical Parenting Plan

Daily:

  • Spend 10–20 minutes in one-on-one conversation with your child.

  • Offer encouragement and praise for effort and honesty.

Weekly:

  • Share at least one family meal around the table without distractions.

  • Read a Bible story or PNG proverb and discuss its meaning.

Monthly:

  • Visit your child’s school and talk with their teachers.

  • Teach a skill such as cooking, gardening, or sewing.

  • Take a walk or outing with your child to strengthen your bond.

Yearly:

  • Sit down and reflect on your family’s growth.

  • Set goals together for the new year.

  • Celebrate achievements—big or small.

Chapter 10: Final Words – The Seeds of Change

You don’t need to be perfect to be a good parent. You need to be willing. Willing to learn, to grow, to try again.

Every small act of love, wisdom, and correction adds up to a transformed life. When we raise better children, we shape a better nation.

Let us raise children who will:

  • Think with wisdom

  • Speak with kindness

  • Work with honesty

  • Lead with integrity

Together, we can change Papua New Guinea—one parent, one child, one home at a time.

“Blessed is the nation whose God is the Lord, the people He chose for His inheritance.” — Psalm 33:12
